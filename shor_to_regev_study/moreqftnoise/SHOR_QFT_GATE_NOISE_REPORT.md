# Shor QFT gate-level noise robustness report

Freeze identifier: `shor-qft-gate-noise-v1`. Generated by
`run_shor_qft_gate_noise.py`; raw artifacts in `shor_qft_gate_noise/`.

## Research question

With the inverse QFT executed at full distance, does gate-level noise —
controlled-phase miscalibration, single-qubit RZ over/under-rotation, or CX
depolarizing — degrade verified order or factor recovery, and at what shot
budget does degradation first surface?

## Design

Eight held-out `(N, base)` instances, 16 noise settings (a zero-noise baseline
plus three strengths of each of five axes), shot budgets `{1, 2, 8, 16}`, and
48 paired-seed realizations per cell. The inverse-QFT distance is fixed at its
exact cutoff so that all signal is attributable to imperfect gates, not dropped
layers. The continued-fraction decoder never receives the true order or the
factors. Row counts: 512 configuration, 512 per-instance, 24,576 trial, 60
paired-difference rows.

## Distribution-level effect of each axis

The one-shot phase-law TV to the exact distribution grows smoothly with each
axis (maxima over the holdout):

| Axis (strongest tested) | Max one-shot TV | Min Hellinger affinity |
|---|---:|---:|
| Controlled-phase gain `g=0.10` | 0.0783 | 0.972 |
| Controlled-phase scatter `sigma=0.10` | 0.0746 | 0.967 |
| RZ bias `b=0.10` | 0.0737 | 0.984 |
| RZ scatter `sigma=0.10` | 0.0316 | 0.988 |
| CX depolarizing `p=0.01` | 0.8613 | 0.389 |

The transpiled inverse QFT carries 150–203 CX gates over the holdout, so a per-CX
depolarizing rate of `p = 0.01` already routes 78–87% of the output mass to the
uniform law — by far the most destructive axis at the distribution level.

## Recovery result

Despite large distribution-level perturbations, recovery is highly shot-robust.
At `shots >= 8`, **all 15 noise settings are non-inferior at margin 0.10** for
both order and factor recovery; every held-out instance still recovers with
probability 1.0 even under `p = 0.01` CX depolarizing. The continued-fraction
decoder only needs one good phase in the batch, and eight shots almost always
supply one.

The gate-noise gradient becomes visible only in the **single-shot** regime,
where no setting is non-inferior at 0.10 and the axes separate cleanly by worst
held-out instance:

| Setting | Order mean Δ | Order min Δ | Factor mean Δ |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| `cx_depol_0p010` | −0.122 | −0.354 | −0.146 |
| `cx_depol_0p005` | −0.091 | −0.313 | −0.107 |
| `cphase_gain_0p05` | −0.063 | −0.208 | −0.016 |
| `rz_bias_0p05` | −0.047 | −0.125 | −0.021 |
| `cphase_gain_0p02` | −0.036 | −0.125 | −0.026 |

CX depolarizing dominates the single-shot degradation, followed by coherent
controlled-phase gain, then RZ bias; the per-gate Gaussian-scatter variants are
the mildest at matched strength because independent zero-mean errors partially
average out over the many gates of the QFT.

## Interpretation

The result mirrors the truncation study's empirical saturation from a different
direction: realistic inverse-QFT gate noise does not threaten Shor recovery at
any practical shot budget on these instances, because the continued-fraction
decoder tolerates a badly distorted phase law as long as one clean phase lands.
The single-shot endpoint is the discriminating regime, and there the ranking is
unambiguous — two-qubit depolarizing is the axis that matters, coherent
controlled-phase miscalibration second, single-qubit RZ rotation error least.

This is a task/state-specific robustness statement for the eight held-out
semiprimes, not a universal claim: the CX-depolarizing axis uses a global
depolarizing surrogate keyed to the transpiled two-qubit count, which
upper-bounds the mixing an actual local channel produces.
