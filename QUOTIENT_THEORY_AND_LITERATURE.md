# Quotient theory and primary-literature audit for Regev-style factoring

## Status and scope

This memo isolates the mathematics needed to discuss removing already-known
non-factor-yielding relations from Regev-style lattice post-processing. It
does not report an empirical result and does not claim a new factoring
algorithm.

Several statements below are immediate consequences of standard abelian-group
and lattice theory. They are included because an implementation that confuses
an exact group quotient with Euclidean projection can silently delete the
factor-bearing information. The literature discussion is a targeted audit of
primary sources, not proof that no related method exists.

## 1. Roots, squared bases, and the two relation lattices

Let \(N>2\), and let

\[
B=(b_1,\ldots,b_d)
\]

be an ordered family of specifically selected units modulo \(N\). Each root
remains permanently paired with its circuit base

\[
a_i=b_i^2\pmod N.
\]

Define

\[
\beta_B:\mathbb Z^d\longrightarrow(\mathbb Z/N\mathbb Z)^\times,
\qquad
\beta_B(z)=\prod_{i=1}^d b_i^{z_i}\pmod N,
\]

and

\[
h_A:\mathbb Z^d\longrightarrow(\mathbb Z/N\mathbb Z)^\times,
\qquad
h_A(z)=\prod_{i=1}^d a_i^{z_i}=\beta_B(z)^2\pmod N.
\]

The squared-base relation lattice is

\[
L=\ker h_A
=\left\{z\in\mathbb Z^d:\prod_i a_i^{z_i}=1\pmod N\right\}.
\]

The root-dependent sublattice is

\[
L_0
=\left\{z\in L:\beta_B(z)\in\{1,-1\}\pmod N\right\}.
\]

These objects must not be conflated:

- \(B\) contains the selected roots used in factor extraction.
- \(A\) contains the squared residues seen by the quantum arithmetic.
- \(L\) depends only on \(A\).
- \(L_0\) depends on the retained roots \(B\).

### Direct-factor-useful and direct-factor-useless relations

A relation \(z\in L\setminus L_0\) is **direct-factor-useful** in this memo.
Then \(\beta_B(z)\) is a nontrivial square root of unity, and

\[
\gcd(\beta_B(z)-1,N)
\quad\text{and}\quad
\gcd(\beta_B(z)+1,N)
\]

yield proper divisors in the usual odd, non-prime-power factoring setting.

A relation \(z\in L_0\) is **direct-factor-useless** only in the narrow sense
that applying those two GCDs to that relation does not directly split \(N\).
This does not mean that \(L_0\) relations contain no geometric or
reconstruction information.

For a norm bound \(T\), define

\[
\mathcal S_T(L)=\{z\in L\setminus\{0\}:\|z\|_2\le T\},
\]

\[
\mathcal S_T(L_0)=\mathcal S_T(L)\cap L_0,
\qquad
\mathcal F_T(B)=\mathcal S_T(L)\setminus L_0.
\]

## 2. The sample-augmented lattice and its exact norm

Suppose \(m\) Fourier samples are represented by rows

\[
w_1,\ldots,w_m\in[0,1)^d,
\]

and let \(W\in\mathbb R^{m\times d}\) have rows \(w_j^T\). For \(S>0\),
Regev's augmented lattice is generated by the **columns** of

\[
\mathcal B_S=
\begin{pmatrix}
I_d&0\\
SW&SI_m
\end{pmatrix}.
\]

Thus

\[
\Lambda(W,S)
=\left\{
\begin{pmatrix}z\\S(Wz+q)\end{pmatrix}
:z\in\mathbb Z^d,\ q\in\mathbb Z^m
\right\}.
\]

For fixed projected vector \(z\), the shortest possible augmented squared norm
is

\[
c_W(z)^2
=\|z\|_2^2
+S^2\sum_{j=1}^m
\operatorname{dist}(\langle w_j,z\rangle,\mathbb Z)^2.
\]

This follows by minimizing each coordinate of \(Wz+q\) over
\(q_j\in\mathbb Z\).

When \(w_j=k_j/D\) and \(S=p/s\) is rational, clearing denominators gives the
exact integer column basis

\[
sD\,\mathcal B_S=
\begin{pmatrix}
sD I_d&0\\
pK&pD I_m
\end{pmatrix},
\]

where \(K\) has rows \(k_j^T\). A row-basis LLL or BKZ implementation must use
the transpose and track the common scale.

The augmented lattice also contains vectors whose first \(d\) coordinates are
not in \(L\). Every projected candidate must first be checked with
\(h_A(z)=1\), and its \(L_0\) status must then be tested with the retained
roots through \(\beta_B(z)\).

## 3. Exact quotienting by verified \(L_0\) relations

Let \(U\le L_0\) be generated by relations whose membership in both \(L\) and
\(L_0\) has been checked exactly.

### Theorem 1: exact preservation of the factor target

There is an exact sequence

\[
0\longrightarrow L_0/U
\longrightarrow L/U
\overset{\varphi}{\longrightarrow}L/L_0
\longrightarrow0,
\]

where

\[
\varphi(z+U)=z+L_0.
\]

Consequently,

\[
\boxed{(L/U)/(L_0/U)\cong L/L_0.}
\]

#### Proof

The map is well-defined because \(U\subseteq L_0\). It is surjective because
every class \(z+L_0\) has preimage \(z+U\). Its kernel consists precisely of
the classes \(z+U\) with \(z\in L_0\), namely \(L_0/U\). The first or third
isomorphism theorem gives the result. In particular,

\[
z\in L\setminus L_0
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
z+U\notin L_0/U.
\]

This is standard group theory, not a novel factoring result.

### Lift invariance of factor extraction

If \(z'\) and \(z\) lift the same class in \(L/U\), then \(z'=z+u\) for some
\(u\in U\subseteq L_0\). Hence

\[
\beta_B(z')=\beta_B(z)\beta_B(u)=\pm\beta_B(z).
\]

Changing the sign only swaps the two GCDs. Therefore

\[
\left\{
\gcd(N,\beta_B(z)-1),
\gcd(N,\beta_B(z)+1)
\right\}
\]

is invariant as an unordered pair under the choice of lift. This too is an
elementary consequence of \(U\subseteq L_0\).

## 4. Two-torsion and the saturation hazard

For every \(z\in L\),

\[
\beta_B(2z)=\beta_B(z)^2=h_A(z)=1.
\]

Thus

\[
\boxed{2L\subseteq L_0,}
\]

and

\[
\Gamma=L/L_0
\]

has exponent at most two. The factor-bearing information is therefore
two-primary information.

Define the saturation relative to \(L\) by

\[
\operatorname{Sat}_L(U)
=\{x\in L:\exists r\ge1,\;rx\in U\}
=L\cap\operatorname{span}_{\mathbb R}U.
\]

Then

\[
\operatorname{Tor}(L/U)
\cong\operatorname{Sat}_L(U)/U.
\]

Indeed, \(x+U\) is torsion exactly when a nonzero multiple of \(x\) lies in
\(U\).

If the inclusion of \(U\) in \(L\) has Smith invariant factors
\(s_1,\ldots,s_r\), then

\[
L/U\cong
\mathbb Z^{d-r}\oplus
\bigoplus_{i=1}^r\mathbb Z/s_i\mathbb Z.
\]

Discarding the finite components is invalid: an even-order Smith component may
carry exactly the nonzero class in \(L/L_0\).

### Safe odd saturation

The odd saturation

\[
\operatorname{Sat}_{L,\mathrm{odd}}(U)
=\{x\in L:\exists\text{ odd }r,\;rx\in U\}
\]

is contained in \(L_0\). In the exponent-two group \(L/L_0\), odd
multiplication satisfies \(r[x]=[x]\). Since \(rx\in U\subseteq L_0\), it
follows that \([x]=0\). No analogous safety statement holds for even
saturation.

## 5. Euclidean projection is not the exact quotient

Let

\[
V=\operatorname{span}_{\mathbb R}U,
\]

let \(\pi:\mathbb R^d\to V^\perp\) be orthogonal projection, and put

\[
S_U=L\cap V=\operatorname{Sat}_L(U).
\]

Then

\[
\ker(\pi|_L)=S_U,
\qquad
\pi(L)\cong L/S_U.
\]

More importantly,

\[
\boxed{
\pi(L)/\pi(L_0)\cong L/(L_0+S_U).
}
\]

#### Proof

Map \(L\) to \(\pi(L)/\pi(L_0)\). Its kernel consists of the \(z\) for which
\(\pi(z)=\pi(z_0)\) for some \(z_0\in L_0\). Equivalently,
\(z-z_0\in S_U\), so the kernel is \(L_0+S_U\). The first isomorphism theorem
gives the result.

Projection therefore preserves the factor target exactly if

\[
\boxed{S_U\subseteq L_0.}
\]

Primitivity of \(U\) in \(L\) is sufficient, since then \(S_U=U\). Being
primitive in the ambient lattice \(\mathbb Z^d\) is a stronger sufficient
condition. Neither follows from real linear independence alone.

### Explicit \(N=15\) counterexample

Take

\[
N=15,\qquad d=1,\qquad b_1=4,\qquad a_1=4^2=1\pmod{15}.
\]

Then

\[
L=\mathbb Z,
\qquad
L_0=2\mathbb Z,
\]

because \(4^{2k}=1\), whereas
\(4^{2k+1}=4\notin\{1,14\}\). Let

\[
U=2\mathbb Z=L_0.
\]

The exact quotient

\[
L/U\cong\mathbb Z/2\mathbb Z
\]

retains the odd, factor-bearing class:

\[
\gcd(4-1,15)=3,
\qquad
\gcd(4+1,15)=5.
\]

However,

\[
\operatorname{span}_{\mathbb R}U=\mathbb R,
\qquad
\operatorname{Sat}_L(U)=L.
\]

Orthogonal projection maps all of \(L\) to zero and destroys the factor class.
This disproves any rule that silently replaces the exact quotient by
projection onto the complement of the known directions.

## 6. Projecting dual samples is generally invalid

Even when primal projection is certified safe, projecting Fourier samples does
not automatically produce samples from the dual of the projected lattice.
Relative to \(V^\perp\),

\[
(\pi(L))^*=L^*\cap V^\perp.
\]

But generally

\[
\pi(L^*)\not\subseteq(\pi(L))^*.
\]

For example, take \(L=\mathbb Z^2\) and

\[
V=\operatorname{span}_{\mathbb R}(1,1).
\]

Then

\[
\pi(1,0)=\tfrac12(1,-1),
\]

whereas

\[
(\pi(L))^*=\mathbb Z(1,-1).
\]

Thus the projection of \((1,0)\in L^*\) is not in the dual of the projected
lattice. Reduced-dimensional Regev reconstruction cannot be justified merely
by projecting measured coordinates. One must instead:

- retain an exact quotient-module representation, including torsion;
- deflate inside an exact primal or augmented-lattice construction; or
- prove a separate sampling theorem that produces the correct annihilator or
  restricted-dual distribution.

## 7. A quotient-gap diagnostic

Assume \(S_U\subseteq L_0\), so projection is safe, and define

\[
\bar L=\pi(L),
\qquad
\bar L_0=\pi(L_0).
\]

Let

\[
\lambda_F(U)
=\min\{\|y\|_2:y\in\bar L\setminus\bar L_0\},
\]

and

\[
\lambda_0(U)
=\min\{\|y\|_2:0\ne y\in\bar L_0\}.
\]

For an \(\alpha\)-approximate shortest-vector procedure, define

\[
QG_\alpha(U)
=\log_2\frac{\lambda_0(U)}{\alpha\lambda_F(U)}.
\]

If \(QG_\alpha(U)>0\), then every nonzero vector returned with norm at most
\(\alpha\lambda_1(\bar L)\) lies outside \(\bar L_0\). The inequality implies
\(\lambda_F(U)<\lambda_0(U)\), so
\(\lambda_1(\bar L)=\lambda_F(U)\), while an \(\alpha\)-approximate result is
strictly shorter than every nonzero vector in \(\bar L_0\).

This is an elementary gap argument, not a novelty claim. Exact values require
oracle knowledge of \(L\) and \(L_0\). Bounded enumeration gives a censored
post-hoc statistic and must not serve simultaneously as a selector objective
and recovery endpoint.

For measured samples, the analogous oracle-side diagnostic replaces the
Euclidean norm by \(c_W(z)\). It remains a mechanism diagnostic, not the
sample-to-lattice algorithm.

## 8. Exact factor-blind construction of \(L_0\)

No factorization of \(N\) is required to construct \(L_0\) on sufficiently
small audit instances. Consider

\[
\psi_B:\mathbb Z^d
\longrightarrow
(\mathbb Z/N\mathbb Z)^\times/\{\pm1\},
\qquad
z\longmapsto\beta_B(z)\{\pm1\}.
\]

Then

\[
L_0=\ker\psi_B.
\]

A Cayley traversal of the signed image records one exponent representative
for each signed residue. Every non-tree edge supplies a kernel relation, and
the fundamental edge relations generate the full kernel by telescoping a
closed walk. If \(H_0\) is a full-rank row-Hermite basis of those relations,
the first isomorphism theorem gives the independent check

\[
|\det H_0|
=[\mathbb Z^d:L_0]
=|\operatorname{im}\psi_B|.
\]

This uses modular multiplication and inversion but no supplied factors or
group orders. Cayley enumeration can be exponentially large, so it is an
audit oracle, not scalable factoring post-processing. The local implementation
is in
[quotient_metrics.py](/Users/summermalik/Desktop/regevs/regev_research/quotient_metrics.py).

## 9. Elementary limitations of quotient-only post-processing

The following limitations are elementary consequences of subgroup closure and
quotient geometry. They are not priority or novelty claims.

### 9.1 Recovered-span barrier

Suppose the exactly verified projected relation generators available to a
post-processing procedure are

\[
H=\{h_1,\ldots,h_r\}\subseteq L_0,
\]

and let

\[
\langle H\rangle_{\mathbb Z}
=\left\{\sum_{i=1}^r c_i h_i:c_i\in\mathbb Z\right\}.
\]

Because \(L_0\) is a subgroup,

\[
\boxed{\langle H\rangle_{\mathbb Z}\subseteq L_0.}
\]

Now let \(U\le L_0\). The image of the recovered span in the exact quotient
satisfies

\[
\big(\langle H\rangle_{\mathbb Z}+U\big)/U
\subseteq L_0/U.
\]

Therefore no procedure restricted to:

- integer linear combinations of the recovered generators;
- exact reduction modulo \(U\);
- changes of lift by elements of \(U\); and
- further quotient operations by subgroups already verified inside \(L_0\)

can create a class in

\[
(L/U)\setminus(L_0/U).
\]

#### Proof

Every integer combination of the \(h_i\) lies in \(L_0\). Adding or
subtracting an element of \(U\subseteq L_0\) remains inside \(L_0\). Passing
to the quotient sends those elements into \(L_0/U\). Repeating these
operations cannot leave that subgroup.

This is a span-only barrier, not an impossibility theorem for factoring. It
can be escaped only by supplying information outside the recovered span, for
example:

- a newly recovered generator not in \(L_0\);
- a larger or differently shaped relation search region;
- a new sample batch that changes the reconstructed generator set; or
- a separate algorithm that searches outside
  \(\langle H\rangle_{\mathbb Z}\).

Merely re-expressing, quotienting, or recombining the same \(L_0\) generators
cannot produce a direct-factor-useful relation.

### 9.2 Chosen-section geometry has no free lunch

The exact quotient theorem preserves group classes, but an abstract quotient
does not choose a canonical Euclidean representative for each class. Different
integer sections can give arbitrarily different LLL geometry.

Use the \(N=15\) arithmetic

\[
b=4,\qquad a=b^2=1\pmod{15},
\]

for which

\[
L=\mathbb Z,\qquad L_0=2\mathbb Z.
\]

Form the abstract augmented lattice

\[
\widetilde L=L\oplus\mathbb Z=\mathbb Z^2
\]

with direct-factor-useless sublattice

\[
\widetilde L_0=L_0\oplus\mathbb Z
=2\mathbb Z\oplus\mathbb Z.
\]

Let the primitive nuisance direction be

\[
U=\langle(0,1)\rangle\subseteq\widetilde L_0.
\]

Then

\[
\widetilde L/U\cong\mathbb Z
\]

through the first coordinate. For every integer \(M\), both

\[
C_0=\langle(1,0)\rangle
\quad\text{and}\quad
C_M=\langle(1,M)\rangle
\]

are exact integral complements:

\[
\widetilde L=U\oplus C_0=U\oplus C_M.
\]

They define two valid sections of the same quotient. The useful odd quotient
class has lifts

\[
(1,0)\in C_0
\quad\text{and}\quad
(1,M)\in C_M,
\]

with norms

\[
\|(1,0)\|_2=1,
\qquad
\|(1,M)\|_2=\sqrt{M^2+1}.
\]

Both lifts have first coordinate one and therefore correspond to the same
direct-factor-useful class for \(b=4\). Nevertheless, their norms differ by an
arbitrarily large factor.

Equivalently, the two unimodular row bases

\[
\big((0,1),(1,0)\big)
\quad\text{and}\quad
\big((0,1),(1,M)\big)
\]

contain the same primitive \(U\) row. Simply deleting that row leaves a
length-one quotient generator in the first presentation and a generator of
length \(\sqrt{M^2+1}\) in the second. Once the row has been deleted, rank-one
LLL cannot repair this choice.

This example does not show that quotient methods must fail. It shows that:

- exact class preservation alone supplies no Euclidean-quality guarantee;
- primitive row deletion is not a canonical quotient construction;
- a chosen section, closest-lift rule, projection, or quotient norm is an
  additional algorithmic choice; and
- finding minimum-norm lifts can itself require a closest-vector computation.

Thus an improvement attributed to quotienting must specify and ablate the
section or metric used. The abstract isomorphism theorem alone cannot predict
LLL or BKZ performance.

### 9.3 Same-\(A\) root twists are sample-indistinguishable

Fix the exponent-state preparation, Fourier convention, and circuit bases
\(A=(a_1,\ldots,a_d)\). The modular arithmetic evaluates

\[
h_A(x)=\prod_i a_i^{x_i}\pmod N.
\]

Consequently, the ideal quantum state after modular evaluation, the reduced
exponent-register state, and the Fourier-sample law depend on \(A\), the
state-preparation window, and the noise model. They do not depend on which
stored roots \(B\) were used to generate the same squared residues \(A\).
Equivalently, the period structure available to the sample law is
\(L=\ker h_A\); the root-dependent \(L_0\) is not determined by those data.

The extreme one-dimensional example is

\[
N=15,\qquad a=1.
\]

Two valid retained-root choices are

\[
b=1
\quad\text{and}\quad
b'=4,
\]

because

\[
1^2\equiv4^2\equiv1\pmod{15}.
\]

For both choices,

\[
h_A(x)=1
\quad\text{and}\quad
L=\mathbb Z,
\]

so every circuit and every sample distribution that uses only \(A\) is
identical.

For \(b=1\),

\[
L_0=\mathbb Z,
\]

and there is no direct-factor-useful class. For \(b'=4\),

\[
L_0'=2\mathbb Z,
\]

and every odd relation is useful, since

\[
\gcd(4-1,15)=3,
\qquad
\gcd(4+1,15)=5.
\]

Therefore no statistic computed solely from \(N\), \(A\), \(L\), or the
quantum samples can determine the \(L_0\) classification for both retained
root families. This is an identifiability limitation for residue-only or
sample-only diagnostics, not an obstruction to a correct implementation:
Regev's formulation begins with the chosen roots, and correct
post-processing retains and uses them.

## 11. Novelty boundaries

### Standard or already known

The following should not be presented as new:

- the definitions of \(L\), \(L_0\), and the need for a vector in
  \(L\setminus L_0\);
- retaining the chosen roots for factor extraction;
- the exact-sequence and third-isomorphism argument;
- lift invariance under addition of an \(L_0\) vector;
- the exponent-two property of \(L/L_0\);
- the relationship between saturation and torsion;
- the projection-kernel calculation;
- the dual identity \((\pi L)^*=L^*\cap V^\perp\);
- the use of a relative shortest factor-bearing length;
- replacing LLL by BKZ as a stronger reduction comparator.

### Not located in the targeted audit

The audit did not locate a Regev factoring method that combines all of:

- exact verification of recovered \(L_0\) relations;
- explicit preservation of Smith torsion;
- a saturation certificate before Euclidean dimension reduction;
- repeated deflation inside the actual augmented-lattice endpoint; and
- fresh-sample or sample-split evaluation that accounts for every additional
  quantum sample.

This is not a novelty finding. It identifies only a narrowly specified method
whose prior-art status remains unresolved.

### Evidence required before a research claim

A defensible algorithmic claim would require:

- an exact construction that never discards unverified even-primary torsion;
- proof that its manipulation of samples preserves the required dual-sampling
  model, or a design operating only on an exact primal/augmented lattice;
- comparisons against plain LLL, fixed-block BKZ, and
  Ragavan–Vaikuntanathan corrupt-sample filtering;
- held-out composites with \(N\) as the unit of generalization;
- fresh sample acquisition or strict sample splitting after \(U\) is learned;
- factor extraction using only roots paired with the selected bases; and
- complete circuit-call and classical-runtime accounting.

Until those conditions are met, quotient-aware deflation is a mathematical
design hypothesis, not a verified improvement to Regev's factoring algorithm.
